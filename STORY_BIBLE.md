GAME STORY BIBLE (v2)
=====================
Title: The Sleeping World
A browser-based RPG for Leo (11), Walter (9), and James (6)

A note on tone and intention: This story is written in the tradition of
Tolkien -- not allegory in any direct sense, but a tale whose shape is
formed by Catholic imagination. Sacrifice, restoration, vocation, the
nature of evil as absence rather than force, and the communion of persons
are structural to the story. None of this needs to be named. It only
needs to be felt.

════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
CHARACTERS
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

LEO (Player 1 / Paladin-Tank)
- Age: 11, the oldest
- Role: Leader, shield, front-line fighter
- Personality: Loud, friendly, outgoing, feels the weight of responsibility
- Mechanic flavor: High defense, rally/buff abilities, can taunt enemies
  to protect his brothers
- Arc: Learns that being the leader doesn't mean doing it all himself.
  His defining moment is not a charge but a choice to hold the line
  while his brothers do what only they can do.
- Fear (used against him by the Nightmare King): That he has already
  failed his brothers. That the evidence is there if he looks.
- Fear redeemed: He hasn't failed them. He has been there the whole
  time. Presence is its own form of protection.

WALTER (Player 2 / Gadgeteer-Inventor)
- Age: 9, the middle
- Role: Support, healer, problem-solver
- Personality: Smart, analytical, wants to understand how everything works
- Mechanic flavor: Builds/deploys gadgets, heals brothers, can "scan"
  enemies for weaknesses
- Arc: Learns that not everything has a logical solution -- sometimes you
  have to trust your brothers. His intelligence is never a punchline.
  His growth is emotional, not intellectual.
- Fear (used against him by the Nightmare King): A problem he cannot
  solve. Absolute helplessness.
- Fear redeemed: He couldn't solve it alone. They solved it together.
  That is not failure. That is the point.

JAMES (Player 3 / Trickster)
- Age: 6, the youngest
- Role: Wild card, rogue, chaos agent
- Personality: Fearless, unpredictable, thinks everything is an adventure
- Mechanic flavor: Sneak attacks, taunts/distracts enemies, wild-card
  abilities with unpredictable effects
- Arc: His fearlessness -- played for laughs early -- is revealed as
  something real and rare. But it is not sufficient on its own. The
  Nightmare King cannot read him, but it takes all three brothers
  together to actually unmake him.
- Fear (used against him by the Nightmare King): The Nightmare King
  cannot find one. He is genuinely unafraid. This is the crack in the
  King's armor -- but a crack is not a victory. That requires the others.
- Note on balance: James should have one genuinely funny moment per
  dreamscape, but his immunity to fear is a key that unlocks the door --
  Leo and Walter still have to walk through it.


════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
THE VILLAIN
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

THE NIGHTMARE KING
- He lives in the space between sleep and waking
- He has been watching children sleep their whole lives, feeding on
  the fears that live in that dark margin
- His goal: if the Dreamer is imprisoned long enough, the waking world
  freezes permanently. A frozen world feels no hope -- and hope is
  what he cannot consume or survive
- His philosophy: He is not evil for evil's sake. He is a nihilist.
  He genuinely believes that hope is a lie told to children, that the
  frozen world is the honest world, that nothing means anything and he
  is simply the only one willing to say so. He is not wrong that the
  world is hard. He is wrong about what that means.
- His power: He knows each person's secret fear. He uses this to taunt,
  demoralize, and paralyze. Fear is his only real weapon.
- His weakness: He is a creature of absence -- not a force but a void.
  He has no power of his own, only the power given to him by belief.
  He cannot be defeated by strength alone. He is undone when all three
  boys act together in their full natures simultaneously: Leo's
  steadfast courage holding the line, Walter's clarity naming the lie
  for what it is, and James's fearlessness landing the final blow.
  Remove any one of the three and it doesn't work.
- His dissolution: He does not die dramatically. He dissolves -- the
  way a bad dream dissolves at 7am when the light comes through the
  curtains. He was never as solid as he seemed.
- Tone: Genuinely threatening but not grotesque. Mythic and dark.
  Think the Horned King, Chernabog, or the Witch-king -- figures of
  real menace that stop short of nightmare fuel for a six-year-old.


════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
THE DREAMER
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

- An ancient, gentle figure -- luminous, vast, and very old. His form
  is suggested rather than defined. A giant moth. Soft light in the
  shape of a man. Something that feels like it has always been there.
- He is not a god, but he is old beyond counting. He has been dreaming
  the world's dreams since before memory.
- He cannot fight or save himself -- he can only dream. That is both
  his power and his limitation.
- Crucial detail: He could have resisted capture. The Nightmare King
  expected him to fight back. He did not. He accepted captivity
  because struggling would have shattered the dream-fragments entirely
  and the world would have frozen at once. His captivity is an act of
  love, not defeat. The boys do not rescue a victim. They complete
  what he began.
- When the boys free him, he does not speak in words. He shows each
  boy a dream -- not of their own future happiness, but of someone they
  will help someday. A person they haven't met. A moment they won't
  understand for years. The gift he gives is not reward but vocation:
  a purpose placed quietly inside them before they are ready for it.
- He is gone before they can thank him.


════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
THE GUIDE
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

DUSTY (the Moth)
- A small, ancient moth. Dry, a little cranky, and waiting with the
  patience of something that has been waiting a very long time.
- She was sent by the Dreamer before he was taken -- he knew what was
  coming and prepared anyway. She has been carrying her message since
  before the boys' grandfather was born.
- She cannot fight. She gives clues, lore, and sarcastic commentary.
- She is a figure of fidelity, not power. She stayed. That is her
  holiness, and it is entirely unglamorous.
- James names her Dusty in the first five minutes. She hates it.
  The game never calls her anything else.
- Her one genuine emotional moment comes in Dreamscape 3, after the
  Nightmare King speaks to the boys for the first time. She says,
  quietly: "I have been carrying this since before your grandfather
  was born. I am glad it was you." Then she goes back to being cranky.
  It is not referenced again. It just sits there.


════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
THE WORLD
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The Dream Realm is not one place. It is a sequence of broken
dreamscapes -- fragments of the Dreamer's mind that the Nightmare King
has warped and frozen. Each one is a piece of what the world was meant
to be, corrupted but not destroyed. The boys do not win these places.
They restore them. There is a difference.

The shards of the Dreamer's light are not power-ups or collectibles.
They are acts of restoration. When a shard is recovered, something
broken returns to what it was always meant to be. The language around
this should feel like healing, not victory. Things are made whole.
That is the shape of the world in this story: fundamentally good,
in need of restoration, always capable of returning to itself.


════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
STORY BEAT SHEET
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

── ACT ONE: THE STILLNESS ──────────────────────────────────

BEAT 1 — OPENING SCENE: THE FROZEN MORNING
  The game opens in the boys' house. Normal morning chaos. Then
  everything stops. Parents mid-sentence. Toast frozen in the air.
  The dog stopped mid-scratch. Only the three boys can move.
  Tone: Eerie but not scary. Uncanny rather than horrifying.
  The world looks exactly like itself -- it is just perfectly, wrongly
  still.
  Player action: Explore the house. Find that the whole neighborhood
  is the same. No wind. No birds. No sound at all.

BEAT 2 — DUSTY APPEARS
  The moth lands on the kitchen table. She announces, with some
  impatience, that she has been waiting for them to notice her for
  several minutes now. She delivers the exposition:
  - The Dreamer has been taken. He did not resist, which was the
    point -- resistance would have ended everything at once.
  - The waking world runs on the Dreamer's dreaming. Without him,
    it is freezing solid. They have until sunrise.
  - The only way in is through a dream door, which she can open.
  - The boys can cross because children still remember how. That is
    not a metaphor. It is simply true.
  James names her Dusty immediately. She does not dignify this.

BEAT 3 — THE DREAM DOOR
  Dusty opens a door at the back of a closet. Through it: the first
  dreamscape. The boys step through. There is no going back until
  the Dreamer is free.
  Tutorial begins here. Basic movement, combat, and mechanics are
  introduced naturally in the first dreamscape.
  The door closes behind them. The closet is just a closet again.


── ACT TWO: THE DREAMSCAPES ────────────────────────────────

Each dreamscape is a self-contained zone with its own visual tone,
enemies, a puzzle, a mini-boss, and a shard to restore. Each one
favors a different brother's strengths -- the design intention is
that each boy has one dreamscape where he is clearly the key, and
all three are needed everywhere.

DREAMSCAPE 1 — THE ETERNAL AUTUMN FOREST
  Tone: Beautiful and melancholy. Leaves always falling, never
  landing. Amber light that goes on forever. A place that was once
  full of life, now suspended between seasons.
  This dreamscape favors: WALTER
  Enemies: Hollow creatures -- animals made of shadow, moving on
  instinct with no dream left inside them. They are not evil. They
  are lost.
  Puzzle/Gimmick: The forest loops. The boys keep ending up where
  they started. Walter has to scan the environment and identify the
  pattern -- a subtle wrongness in the trees, a path that bends
  where it shouldn't. His analytical nature is the key.
  Mini-Boss: The Stag -- once a great dream-creature, now hollow,
  enormous, and confused. Leo holds the line and draws its attention.
  Walter scans and identifies the shard of light trapped in its chest.
  James creates enough chaos and distraction that Walter gets a clear
  moment to act.
  Resolution: The shard is recovered. The Stag does not die -- it
  collapses peacefully, restored to itself, and the forest breathes
  again. Leaves land. The light changes.
  Emotional beat: Walter says something quiet about how the Stag was
  just confused and trapped. First hint that the Nightmare King's
  victims are not evil -- just lost. Things separated from what they
  were meant to be.
  Hidden image: At the edge of the restored forest, before the boys
  move on, there is a figure -- barely visible -- sitting under a tree.
  An old dreamer who gave something up long ago so this forest could
  exist. He is asleep. The world around him is a little more beautiful
  than it would have been otherwise. Nobody remembers him. Walter
  notices. The game does not explain it. It just sits there.

DREAMSCAPE 2 — THE UPSIDE-DOWN CITY
  Tone: Disorienting and comic. A full city built on the underside of
  a cloud. Gravity is negotiable. Everything is inverted and the
  residents, such as they are, find this completely normal.
  This dreamscape favors: JAMES
  Enemies: Dream-bureaucrats -- officious shadow figures who obstruct
  the boys with rules, forms, and procedure rather than violence.
  They are maddening rather than dangerous. They cannot be fought.
  They have to be outmaneuvered.
  Puzzle/Gimmick: In a world where logic is already broken, James's
  wild-card abilities operate at full strength. His unpredictability
  is not a glitch here -- it is the correct tool. Leo and Walter
  find their usual approaches frustrated at every turn.
  Mini-Boss: The Mayor -- a pompous, hollow figure who has convinced
  the city to stop dreaming altogether. "Dreaming is inefficient,"
  he says. He is not threatening. He is exhausting.
  James leads the resolution here, but Leo has to physically hold
  open the door while it happens, and Walter has to identify which
  of the Mayor's seventeen procedural objections is the one that
  actually matters. All three are needed.
  Emotional beat: Leo gets frustrated. He wants to fight something
  and there is nothing to punch. Walter has to talk him down. It is
  uncomfortable for Leo, and that discomfort is the point. Leading
  does not always mean charging.
  James gets at least one moment of pure, genuine comedy here. Let
  him be six years old at maximum volume.

DREAMSCAPE 3 — THE DRY OCEAN
  Tone: Haunting and vast. An ocean floor with no water. Ships
  settled in the sand. Sea creatures drifting through the air as
  though still swimming, slow and ancient. Coral cities, empty.
  A world that was full once and remembers it.
  This dreamscape favors: LEO
  Enemies: Drowned knights -- armored figures who sank long ago and
  never stopped patrolling. They follow orders that no longer exist
  from a commander who is long gone. They are not evil. They are
  faithful to a purpose that has been lost.
  Puzzle/Gimmick: The water must be restored. Scattered across the
  ocean floor are dream-tears -- crystallized tears from sleepers
  in the real world, preserved here. Collecting them begins to
  refill the ocean, slowly, from the lowest point up. The process
  takes patience. Leo's steadiness is what keeps the group moving
  when it feels like nothing is working.
  Mini-Boss: The Admiral -- the greatest of the drowned knights,
  still waiting for orders that will never come. He is not hostile
  until approached. He challenges the boys: who sent them, and by
  what authority do they restore what was taken?
  Leo answers. He doesn't have a grand speech. He just says they're
  here because someone has to be. The Admiral stands aside.
  Resolution: The ocean refills. The knights, freed from their
  vigil, drift upward slowly -- returned to something like rest.
  The coral cities glow.
  Emotional beat: The Nightmare King speaks for the first time --
  not in person, as a voice. He addresses each boy privately,
  naming their fear. Leo hears: "You've already failed them once.
  You know it." It rattles him.
  Then Dusty's moment: "I have been carrying this since before your
  grandfather was born. I am glad it was you." She goes back to
  being cranky. It is not discussed again.

DREAMSCAPE 4 — THE PALACE OF MIRRORS
  Tone: Psychological and tense. The Nightmare King's outer
  fortress. Every surface is mirror. The reflections do not show
  the boys as they are -- they show them as their fears imagine
  them. Smaller. Failing. Alone.
  This dreamscape favors: ALL THREE EQUALLY
  Enemies: Mirror-selves -- shadow versions of each boy that copy
  their abilities back at them. They cannot be fought directly.
  They match every move.
  Puzzle/Gimmick: The mirror-self copies what you do. The solution
  is always unexpected behavior -- acting against your own instinct.
  Leo's mirror-self expects aggression; Leo has to hold still.
  Walter's mirror-self expects analysis; Walter has to act without
  thinking. James's mirror-self barely exists -- it flickers and
  can't keep up. But James cannot beat the others' mirrors alone.
  The brothers have to act in concert, each suppressing his natural
  instinct and trusting the others to fill the gap. This is the
  hardest thing the game asks of them.
  Emotional beat: Walter freezes in front of his mirror. His fear
  is right there: a problem he cannot solve, staring at him in his
  own face. Leo physically pulls him away from it. "Don't look at
  it. Look at me." Walter's moment: "I don't have to solve
  everything. I just have to keep moving."

THE NIGHTMARE KING'S THRESHOLD
  A long bridge between the Palace and the final tower.
  The Nightmare King walks out to meet them. He does not fight yet.
  He talks. He is calm and certain and very good at this.
  He tells them it's over. He names their fears again, each one
  precisely. He explains, not cruelly but almost gently, that hope
  is a comfort invented for children who can't face the truth.
  He looks at James last. Reaches toward him with his full power.
  Nothing happens. He tries again. Still nothing.
  He withdraws his hand. For the first time, he seems uncertain.
  Not afraid -- uncertain. The first crack.
  He retreats into the tower. The boys look at each other.
  Leo: "Let's go."


── ACT THREE: THE DREAMER ──────────────────────────────────

BEAT — THE TOWER
  Final dungeon. The Nightmare King deploys everything -- all fears,
  all mirrors, waves of hollow creatures. He is not panicking. He
  is thorough.
  Leo holds the line. Walter keeps them standing. James causes
  absolute, targeted chaos. None of them alone would make it through.
  Together they do.

BEAT — THE FINAL BATTLE
  Three phases. Each phase requires a different brother to lead,
  and none of them can win it without the other two holding.

  PHASE 1 — THE FEARS
  The Nightmare King attacks as fear itself. He shapeshifts,
  cycling through each boy's deepest fear made visible.
  Leo and Walter struggle. Their fears are real and he is skilled.
  James is unreadable to him and becomes the anchor in this phase --
  not the attacker, but the fixed point the others hold onto.
  James's role here: stability, not glory. He holds his brothers'
  hands (literally or figuratively -- this can be a mechanic) and
  the fears lose some of their edge.

  PHASE 2 — THE ARGUMENT
  The Nightmare King stops fighting physically. He makes his case.
  He is intelligent and he has had a long time to think about this.
  He is not ranting. He is explaining, almost sadly, why hope is
  a lie and why the frozen world is simply honest.
  Walter is the one who answers. Not with a counter-argument --
  he doesn't have one that matches the Nightmare King's logic.
  Instead Walter names the flaw: "You've been watching us sleep
  our whole lives and you still don't know us at all."
  This phase ends not with a killing blow but with the Nightmare
  King's certainty beginning to crack. He expected fear or force.
  He did not expect to be seen clearly and found wanting.
  Walter's role here: clarity. Naming the lie. This is his moment.

  PHASE 3 — THE UNMAKING
  The Nightmare King is now operating from a damaged position --
  his certainty is gone, his power over James nonexistent, his
  argument answered. He is still enormous and dangerous, but he
  is no longer inevitable.
  Leo steps forward. Not to fight -- to hold. He places himself
  between his brothers and the Nightmare King and simply does not
  move. His fear says he has already failed. He is here anyway.
  That is the answer to his fear, demonstrated rather than spoken.
  With Leo holding, Walter guiding, and James finally unleashed --
  all three acting simultaneously in their full natures -- the
  Nightmare King comes apart. Not dramatically. The way a bad
  dream dissolves at 7am when light comes through the curtains.
  He was never as solid as he seemed.
  The final blow belongs to all three. There is no single hero.

BEAT — THE DREAMER IS FREED
  The tower collapses gently. The Dreamer rises -- vast, luminous,
  ancient. His form is whatever feels right: moth, man, light. He
  does not speak in words.
  He shows each boy a dream. The dreams are not shown to the player.
  The screen holds on each boy's face, lit by something warm.
  The dreams are not of their own futures. They are of someone each
  boy will help someday -- a stranger, a moment, something they
  won't understand for years. Vocation, given quietly, in the dark,
  before they are ready for it.
  Then the Dreamer is gone. The boys didn't get to thank him.

BEAT — HOME
  The dream door opens onto the back of the closet.
  The boys step through. The closet smells like their house.
  Dusty is gone. No goodbye. On the kitchen floor: one small,
  dusty wing-scale. That's all.
  They walk into the kitchen.
  Mom is finishing her sentence. Dad is pouring coffee. The dog
  finishes scratching. The toast lands.
  No one knows. The world is exactly as it was, except it isn't.
  The boys look at each other.
  Fade to the title card.
  End.


════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
BALANCE NOTES: THE THREE BROTHERS
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Each dreamscape is designed to foreground one brother without
diminishing the others. The rough shape:

  Dreamscape 1 (Eternal Autumn Forest): Walter leads
  Dreamscape 2 (Upside-Down City): James leads
  Dreamscape 3 (Dry Ocean): Leo leads
  Dreamscape 4 (Palace of Mirrors): All three equally
  Final Battle Phase 1: James anchors
  Final Battle Phase 2: Walter speaks
  Final Battle Phase 3: Leo holds / all three strike together

The Nightmare King is defeated by the combination -- not by the
youngest's fearlessness alone, not by the oldest's courage alone,
not by the middle's intelligence alone. Remove any one of the three
and it does not work. This should be legible to the players. They
should feel that they each mattered, specifically, in the way that
only they could.


════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
THEMATIC NOTES (for the developer, not the game text)
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The following themes are structural -- they do not need to be named
in the game. They should be felt.

SACRIFICE WITHOUT BITTERNESS
  The Dreamer accepted captivity. He is not a victim. He made a
  choice. The boys complete what he began. This is the shape of
  intercession: the strong bearing what others cannot, not as
  martyrdom but as love.

RESTORATION, NOT CONQUEST
  The boys do not defeat the dreamscapes. They restore them. The
  world is not broken beyond recognition -- it is good, and it
  needs to be returned to itself. Evil in this story is privation:
  absence of what should be there. The ocean has no water. The
  forest loops forever. The city has stopped dreaming. These are
  not enemy territories to be conquered. They are wounded things
  to be healed.

THE COMMUNION OF THREE
  Three persons, distinct natures, one purpose. None sufficient
  alone. Each essential. The math of the final battle should feel
  inevitable once the player understands this -- of course it took
  all three. How could it have been otherwise?

EVIL AS ABSENCE
  The Nightmare King has no power of his own. Only what is given
  to him by fear. He is not a force -- he is a void. His
  dissolution is not a defeat so much as a revelation: he was
  never as real as he appeared. This is a specific theology of
  evil, and it does not need to be stated. It only needs to be
  enacted.

VOCATION OVER REWARD
  The Dreamer gives each boy not happiness but purpose. A task
  they don't yet understand. They go home carrying something
  invisible. This is the opposite of a treasure chest. It is
  closer to a calling. Children will feel this as mysterious and
  right without knowing why.

UNREMARKED HOLINESS
  The sleeping figure at the edge of the restored forest. The
  Admiral who stood watch long after everyone forgot why. Dusty,
  carrying a message for longer than the boys have been alive.
  The world is full of people who gave something and were not
  remembered. Walter notices. The game does not explain. These
  moments are not puzzles. They are just true.


════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
OPEN QUESTIONS FOR DEVELOPMENT
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

- Does each dreamscape need a full explorable map, or is it more
  linear (room to room)? A hybrid may serve best: a central area
  that can be explored, with linear story beats at key points.
- Is there an inventory/item system, or do abilities evolve through
  story beats? Given the boys' ages, simpler may be better.
- Should the clock (time until full freeze) be visible to the player,
  or felt through environmental changes in the waking world glimpsed
  between dreamscapes?
- Are the mirror-self enemies a full combat mechanic or a puzzle
  sequence? Suggest puzzle -- combat against yourself risks being
  frustrating rather than interesting.
- What does each boy's dream at the end show? These could be
  designed with the real Leo, Walter, and James in mind. Consider
  asking them -- without explaining why -- what they would want to
  do someday that would help someone. Their answers might be better
  than anything written here.
- Dusty's wing-scale on the kitchen floor: should it be interactive?
  A small animation when clicked, or just there, saying nothing?
  Suggest: just there.
